Schooling and Education in Australia

Educational Investment and Academic Outcomes

Educational Spending by State and Territory

This choropleth map displays the educational spending per student across Australian states and territories in 2019. Australia's distribution of educational funding reveals stark regional inequalities, with some territories receiving nearly double the investment of others.

The Northern Territory leads with the highest spending at $28.69 per student, while Victoria receives the lowest allocation at just $15.92 per student. These disparities likely reflect the additional costs of delivering education in remote areas and smaller population centers.

National Performance by Location

These bar charts compare national average numeracy and writing performance across different geographic locations. A concerning achievement gap emerges, with students in major cities scoring approximately 50-60 points higher than their peers in very remote areas across both subjects.

Major cities lead with mean scores of around 530 in numeracy and 505 in writing, while very remote areas struggle with scores around 470 in numeracy and 445 in writing. The consistent pattern across both domains suggests systemic barriers—including teacher shortages, limited resources, and connectivity issues—that disproportionately affect rural and remote students.

Spending vs Academic Performance

This scatter plot examines the relationship between spending per student and mean NAPLAN scores across states and territories. The data reveals a surprising paradox: higher investment doesn't guarantee better outcomes, suggesting that educational quality depends on multiple factors beyond pure financial resources. Factors such as remoteness, socio-economic differences, and varying access to qualified teachers can all influence student performance.

Victoria achieves the highest mean NAPLAN score of 568.37 with the lowest spending of just $15.92 per student, demonstrating exceptional efficiency. Meanwhile, the Northern Territory invests $28.69 per student—nearly 80% more—yet achieves a moderate score of 545.56. This suggests factors like teaching quality, curriculum effectiveness, and socioeconomic contexts may outweigh funding levels in determining student success.

Student Discipline Patterns Over Time

Attendance Rates Over Time

This line chart tracks school attendance rates from 2012 to 2023 across school levels. A persistent gap exists between primary and secondary students, with primary attendance consistently maintaining around 92-93% while secondary attendance hovers around 87-88% throughout the period. Indeed, there appears to be a decline in discipline and engagement as individuals grow older, potentially reflecting increased independence, social factors, and external commitments during secondary schooling.

The COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2022) caused notable disruptions, with both levels experiencing declines—primary attendance dropped to approximately 90% and secondary attendance fell to around 84% during 2021. The 4-5 percentage point gap between school levels persists across all years, suggesting that engagement challenges intensify as students progress through adolescence, possibly due to increased independence, work commitments, or disengagement from traditional schooling.

Suspension Trends by Age Group and Gender

This interactive time series reveals suspension patterns across age groups and genders from 2012 to 2023. A striking gender disparity emerges across all age groups, with male students consistently receiving 2-3 times more suspensions than female students, peaking in the 13-16 years age bracket where males account for approximately 75% of all suspensions. This stable pattern indicates behavioral differences or potential bias in disciplinary responses.

Select different age groups to observe how suspension rates escalate dramatically during adolescence—the 13-16 age group shows suspension numbers reaching over 30,000 for males in peak years, compared to around 10,000 for females. The 2020-2021 period shows notable decreases across all groups, likely reflecting pandemic-related school closures and remote learning, with rates rebounding in 2022-2023 as students returned to in-person instruction.

Understanding Suspension Causes

This Sankey diagram maps the flow of suspension incidents in 2013, tracing how suspensions from different year levels aggregate into the total of approximately 66,000 incidents, then branch into specific behavioral categories. The visualization reveals which student groups and behaviors drive disciplinary actions. Years 9-10 contribute the largest share of suspensions, followed by Years 7-8, confirming that early to mid-adolescence represents the peak period for disciplinary issues.

Among behavioral causes, physical violence and aggressive behavior dominate as the leading reasons for suspension, followed by persistent disobedience and substance-related incidents, suggesting that targeted intervention programs focusing on conflict resolution and emotional regulation during the transition to secondary school could significantly reduce suspension rates.
